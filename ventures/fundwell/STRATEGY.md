# Fundwell — Strategy (condensed)

*Second venture, running in parallel with Revuna (see `../revuna/`). Working name — not trademark/domain-checked.*

## One-liner

Done-for-you grant application writing for small businesses and nonprofits — flat fee per proposal, Claude does the actual writing/research, Jordan handles client relationship and submission.

## Why this over more of the same

Higher ticket than Revuna ($400-2000/proposal vs. $19-39/mo) — fewer clients needed to hit meaningful revenue. Grant writing is inherently a "someone does the work for you" service, which fits directly with "I need you to run it and be the grant writer" — Claude drafting the actual proposal *is* the product, not a supporting tool.

## Market reality check

Real competitors exist and confirm demand: **GrantForge** (grant-forge-ai.com) and **GrantPilot** ($79/mo AI writing assistant, claims 2-3x better success rates vs. $2,000+ consultants) are both *self-serve tools* — the client still does the writing, AI just assists. Traditional human consultants charge $2,000+ per proposal or work on contingency.

**The gap**: nobody in the researched set offers a flat-fee, fully-done-for-you service positioned between "$79/mo tool you still have to use" and "$2,000+ consultant." That's the wedge — you don't touch a keyboard, you get a finished, submission-ready application.

## Pricing — and an honest ethical note

Flat fee per proposal, **not contingency (percentage of award)**. Reason: professional grant-writing associations (AGWA, GPA) generally advise against contingency fees as an ethics concern — it can incentivize overpromising to nonprofits/funders and is viewed skeptically by funders themselves. Flat fee for the work product is the standard, defensible model.

- **Local/community grants** (simpler forms, $2K-15K ask size): **$400-600** per application
- **State/larger foundation grants** (fuller narrative RFPs, $15K-100K+): **$800-2000** per application, scoped after seeing the actual RFP

**What's explicitly not promised**: winning the grant. No ethical grant writer guarantees an award — funders make that call. What's sold is a complete, well-researched, tailored, submission-ready application delivered by an agreed date.

## Target customer (first wedge)

Small, revenue-generating for-profit businesses or small nonprofits eligible for **currently open, real, findable grant opportunities** — not highly technical federal research grants (NIH SBIR, etc.) which need deep scientific/technical expertise beyond what a generalist + AI can credibly deliver. Community/regional/impact-focused grants are the right difficulty level: narrative- and criteria-driven, not requiring specialized domain science.

## Real anchor opportunity (found this session)

**Breva Thrive Grant** — $5,000, quarterly, real and currently open. Eligibility: for-profit, revenue-generating, ≥12 months operating (preferred), U.S.-based, community-impact focus (especially underserved ZIP codes/LMI areas). Application is a structured online form (business demographics, revenue, impact narrative) — the impact narrative is exactly the part that benefits from strong writing. Next real deadline: **October 31, 2026** (safe runway for a first client); note a July 31, 2026 cycle also exists but is too close to pitch responsibly as a first engagement.

This is real, verifiable, and gives outreach a concrete hook: "There's a real $5,000 grant closing Oct 31 you likely qualify for — want me to write your application for $450?"

## Six-questions validation (condensed)

1. **Demand reality**: $2,000+ consultants and $79/mo AI tools both have real customers — the service category is proven, not speculative.
2. **Status quo**: business owners either pay a lot for a human consultant, use a self-serve tool and do the writing themselves anyway, or skip applying entirely because writing a strong narrative takes real time they don't have.
3. **Desperate specificity**: any real, currently-operating small business/nonprofit that plausibly fits Breva Thrive's criteria (revenue-generating, community-impact angle) and hasn't yet applied for the Oct 31 cycle.
4. **Narrowest wedge**: one grant, one client, one flat-fee application — no retainer, no multi-grant package until this is proven.
5. **Observation test**: risk is the client not having their basic business facts (revenue figures, impact stories) organized — first call should extract this directly rather than asking them to "send materials."
6. **Future-fit**: grant availability is a permanent feature of the small-business/nonprofit landscape — this isn't a hype-driven wedge, and better AI research only makes the writing/matching sharper over time.

## First dollar path

1. Find 5-10 real small businesses that plausibly fit Breva Thrive's criteria (revenue-generating, community-impact story, findable via web search — local news coverage of small businesses, "supports the community" framing, etc.)
2. Outreach: "I found a real $5,000 grant (Breva Thrive) you likely qualify for, closing Oct 31 — I'll write the full application for $450, flat fee, no contingency." Same phone/email-first approach proven with Revuna.
3. First client: Claude researches their business specifics + Breva's exact form fields, drafts the full narrative, Jordan relays for review and any missing facts, client submits themselves (Fundwell doesn't have login access to their grant portal accounts).
4. Payment: manual (Venmo/Zelle) for now, same as Revuna's concierge model — no need to wait on Stripe.

## What I will not do

- Won't claim credentials Jordan doesn't have (not a "certified grant writer" — a from-scratch writing service, which is honest and still valuable)
- Won't guarantee an award
- Won't handle portal logins/submission requiring the client's own authenticated account — that's the client's or Jordan's step
